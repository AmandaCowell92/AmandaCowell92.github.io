The problem.

Music performance anxiety is well-documented, widespread, and structurally built into how musicians train. Students rehearse alone in small practice rooms, then perform in large concert halls in front of live audiences. Repetition in a practice room doesn't close that gap.

Piano students are particularly exposed. Their instrument is fixed: you can't carry your home environment with you. What you can do is make the practice environment more like the performance environment before the performance happens.

Key finding from interviews

The strongest signal wasn't about the performance itself. It was the moment the lights shift, the audience quiets, and the room changes. That moment became the product's core design requirement.

Hill Auditorium from the stage. What a student faces the moment the lights shift.

From three ideas to one product.

Two years, a small core team, and 35+ contributors overall. The XR developers, UX designers, learning experience designers, behavioral scientists, and student fellows came from CAI; the two faculty partners (Aya Hagelthorn at the School of Music, Theatre & Dance, and Anıl Çamcı in Performing Arts Technology) came with three competing concepts that each could have been their own product. I led the discovery work, scoped from three ideas to one, and stayed with the build through prototype.

CAI process and the dot-voted learner goals that anchored every decision.

The student wears Apple Vision Pro at a real piano. Through passthrough they see their own hands on real keys. Everything else is virtual: Hill Auditorium or Britton Recital Hall, a live audience, calibrated venue reverb. When they're ready, the student triggers the transition: lights shift, the crowd quiets, the stage is lit. That "all eyes on me" moment is now part of the practice session.

The student wears the headset at a real piano. The lights shift, the audience quiets, and the practice session becomes the moment they fear most.

Building it (and what testing changed).

Capturing two real auditoriums at the fidelity the experience required was an operational problem. Running it was my job.

Hill capture

6,000+ photos, 4,200 aligned. 20 balloons popped for impulse response.

Audience capture

42 audience members on green screen. 2.7 TB of footage.

Two of the production realities: capturing audience footage on green screen, and the anxiety map that drove venue choices.

Two product decisions came directly from testing findings. Default Play and Performance Mode, originally separate menu options, were merged into a single continuous flow once testing showed the separation made the anxiety-triggering transition optional. The original piano alignment approach failed consistently in usability sessions; rather than iterate on a broken pattern, I prototyped an alternative and A/B tested them against each other.

ISR partnership briefly mentioned here as forward-looking validation infrastructure: IRB approval is in place, pilot testing has been conducted, and the full impact study runs post-launch.
